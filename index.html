Isaac Malsky

NASA Postdoctoral Fellow · Jet Propulsion Laboratory

I study the weather on planets outside the Solar System.

I'm a NASA Postdoctoral Program Fellow at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory. I build three-dimensional general circulation models of hot Jupiter and sub-Neptune atmospheres, and I develop machine-learning methods that make these simulations orders of magnitude faster, so that whole grids of models can be tested directly against JWST observations.

I earned my PhD in Astronomy & Astrophysics from the University of Michigan in 2024, advised by Emily Rauscher, after a B.A. in Physics from the University of Chicago.

Research

I connect three-dimensional models of exoplanet atmospheres to the spectra and phase curves we observe, increasingly with the help of machine learning. A few representative first-author projects:

arXiv preprint · 2025

Accelerating radiative transfer with transformers

A transformer-based emulator reproduces layer-by-layer radiative fluxes to sub-percent accuracy while running orders of magnitude faster than conventional codes. Large grids of atmospheric models become affordable.

Read the paper
The Astronomical Journal · 2025

Clouds and hazes on GJ 1214 b

Three-dimensional models of GJ 1214 b's metal-rich atmosphere show how clouds and photochemical hazes set its temperature structure, and which scenarios reproduce the planet's JWST phase curve.

Read the paper
The Astrophysical Journal · 2024

How radiative transfer shapes 3D models

Swapping double-gray for multiwavelength radiative transfer changes the winds, temperatures, and spectra a general circulation model predicts, with or without radiatively active clouds.

Read the paper
Nature Astronomy · 2023

Helium-enhanced planets at the radius valley

Photoevaporation can strip hydrogen faster than helium, enriching the envelopes of planets along the upper edge of the radius valley and reshaping their mass–radius relationship.

Read the paper UChicago News
The Astrophysical Journal · 2021

High-resolution emission of cloudy hot Jupiters

High-resolution emission spectra computed from 3D circulation models show that clouds and day–night structure leave detectable fingerprints in non-transiting hot Jupiters.

Read the paper
The Astrophysical Journal · 2020

Atmospheric escape and envelope evolution

Coupling photoevaporative mass loss to the thermal and compositional evolution of planetary envelopes traces how sub-Neptunes shrink and fractionate their atmospheres over gigayears.

Read the paper

Beyond the papers

Observing programs

Co-I on JWST Cycle 5 GO 11301, a 69.6-hour MIRI/LRS phase curve probing silicate clouds in a hot Jupiter, and JWST Cycle 4 GO 7849, 46.3 hours measuring cloudy hot Jupiter counterparts. Co-I on Gemini South and Magellan programs tracing the day–night structure of WASP-121 b and WASP-76 b with high-resolution spectroscopy.

Software

First author of the first public release of RM-GCM, the Rauscher group's general circulation model. My machine-learning emulators for radiative transfer and chemical kinetics are developed openly on GitHub.

Mentoring & service

Mentor to five students, from high school through graduate level. Referee for The Astrophysical Journal, ApJ Letters, and the Journal of Open Source Software.
